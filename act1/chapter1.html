Chapter 1#

Dostoevsky’s protagonists are a whirlwind of contradictions, and you’re right—their psychology often borders on chaos. It’s almost as though they thrive on their own inner turmoil, intoxicated by raw emotion while maintaining an oddly sharp, rational awareness of their irrationality. This lack of tension you’re identifying is fascinating. It’s as if these characters accept their contradictions as fundamental to their being, almost reveling in them. They don’t strive for coherence in the traditional sense, and perhaps that’s why their moral compass isn’t so easily shattered.

In novels like Crime and Punishment, for instance, Raskolnikov’s feverish intellect serves not to resolve his internal battle but to sharpen the knife of his moral dilemmas. It’s a kind of logical madness, where emotion and reason are both fully operational yet not in opposition—more like two blades of a scissor, slicing through the fabric of reality. They aren’t trying to reconcile their instincts with their intellect, they’re using both to propel themselves forward, often to disastrous ends.

In some ways, it reminds me of how music operates—think of how a frenzied, dissonant chord progression can build tension and yet sound complete. It’s not about resolution but the thrill of that tension, much like Dostoevsky’s characters live in a fevered state of being, where the logic itself highlights their madness, but they carry on, almost empowered by it. It’s not immorality but a kind of moral frenzy where traditional boundaries blur.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

def draw_triangle(ax, vertices, labels, color='black'):
    """Draws a triangle given vertices and labels for each vertex."""
    triangle = plt.Polygon(vertices, edgecolor=color, fill=None, linewidth=1.5)
    ax.add_patch(triangle)
    for i, (x, y) in enumerate(vertices):
        ax.text(x, y, labels[i], fontsize=12, ha='center', va='center')

def get_triangle_vertices(center, radius):
    """Returns the vertices of an equilateral triangle centered at `center` with a given `radius`."""
    angles = np.linspace(0, 2 * np.pi, 4)[:-1] + np.pi/2  # angles for vertices of an equilateral triangle
    return np.column_stack([center[0] + radius * np.cos(angles),
                            center[1] + radius * np.sin(angles)])

# Create the plot
fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.set_aspect('equal')

# Define the layers of the fractal with vertices and labels
centers = [(0, 0)]
radii = [1, 2, 3, 4]
triads = [
    ['Betrayal', 'Power', 'Survival'],
    ['Loyalty', 'Empathy', 'Resilience'],
    ['Faith', 'Love', 'Hope'],
    ['Friendship', 'Character', 'Ethics'],

]
colors = ['blue', 'green', 'orange', 'purple']

# Draw the triangles with increasing radius and different colors
for radius, triad, color in zip(radii, triads, colors):
    vertices = get_triangle_vertices(centers[0], radius)
    draw_triangle(ax, vertices, triad, color=color)

# Set limits and hide axes to fit the frame
ax.set_xlim(-3.7, 3.7)
ax.set_ylim(-3.7, 3.7)
ax.axis('off')

# Display the plot
plt.show()
